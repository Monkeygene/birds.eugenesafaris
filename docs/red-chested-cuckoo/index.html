Red-Chested Cuckoofff

Cyanistes caeruleus

The Red-Chested Cuckoo (Cuculus solitarius) is a medium-sized bird known for its distinct call, often heard before it is seen. It features a sleek body with a red-brown chest, contrasting with its grayish upperparts. This species is widely distributed across sub-Saharan Africa and is known for its migratory habits. It prefers dense forests and woodlands, where it feeds mainly on caterpillars and insects.

Habitat & Range

The Red-Chested Cuckoo prefers dense forests, woodlands, and riverine vegetation. It is commonly found in subtropical and tropical regions, where it thrives in areas with plenty of tree cover and a rich insect population.

This species is a partial migrant, with populations in southern Africa moving northwards during the austral winter. Some individuals remain in their breeding territories year-round, while others migrate to central and eastern Africa in search of more favorable conditions.

The Red-Chested Cuckoo is found in various regions across sub-Saharan Africa, including South Africa, Zimbabwe, Botswana, Namibia, Zambia, Malawi, Tanzania, Kenya, and Uganda. It prefers forested and woodland habitats.

The diet of the Red-chested Cuckoo (Cuculus solitarius) is predominantly insectivorous, with a marked preference for lepidopteran larvae, particularly caterpillars—including species that are hairy or toxic and typically avoided by other avian predators. In addition to caterpillars, it consumes a variety of other arthropods such as beetles, orthopterans, and occasionally spiders, foraging primarily within the mid to upper strata of forest and woodland vegetation, where it remains well-concealed among the foliage.

Voice

The Red-Chested Cuckoo is well known for its distinctive call, often described as a repetitive and melodious “piet-my-vrou” sound. This call is a key identifier of the species, particularly during the breeding season when males vocalize frequently to establish territory and attract mates.
